# ISACA IT Audit Methodology Skill

## 1. When to Use / Not Use This Skill

### Use This Skill When:
- Planning or executing an IT audit engagement (IS audit, integrated audit, compliance audit)
- Performing ITGC or ITAC testing and assessment
- Developing a risk-based audit plan or audit universe
- Writing audit observations, findings, or reports using ISACA methodology
- Assessing COBIT 2019 governance or management objectives
- Performing COBIT maturity assessments
- Mapping controls across frameworks (COBIT, COSO, NIST, ISO 27001, AICPA)
- Evaluating IT governance, risk management, or security controls
- Developing audit programs or test procedures
- Performing follow-up on audit findings

### Do NOT Use This Skill When:
- Performing financial statement audits (use AICPA/GAAP methodology instead)
- Providing legal opinions or regulatory compliance certifications
- Replacing professional judgment of certified IS auditors
- Performing penetration testing or vulnerability assessment (use security testing methodology)
- Providing audit opinions without sufficient, reliable, relevant evidence
- Overriding organizational audit charters or policies

### 4.6 COBIT 2019 Information Criteria (7 Criteria)

When evaluating controls, assess them against these 7 information criteria:

| Criterion | Definition | What to Test |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Effectiveness** | Information relevant and pertinent; timely, correct, consistent, usable | Does information support business process needs? |
| **Efficiency** | Optimal use of resources to produce information | Are resources used economically? |
| **Confidentiality** | Protection from unauthorized disclosure | Is data protected from unauthorized access? |
| **Integrity** | Accuracy, completeness, and validity per business values | Is data accurate and complete? |
| **Availability** | Information available when required, now and future | Is information accessible when needed? |
| **Compliance** | Adherence to laws, regulations, contracts, internal policies | Are regulatory requirements met? |
| **Reliability** | Appropriate information for fiduciary and governance responsibilities | Can management rely on information for decisions? |

### 4.7 COBIT 2019 Design Factors

When tailoring a COBIT governance system, apply these design factors:

1. **Enterprise Strategy** — Cost optimization, growth, agility, compliance, digital transformation
2. **Enterprise Goals Cascade** — 17 enterprise goals → alignment goals → 40 governance/management objectives
3. **Risk Profile** — Risk appetite, risk tolerance, enterprise risk posture
4. **IT-Related Issues** — Current pain points and strategic IT challenges
5. **Threat Landscape** — External threats, industry-specific risks, emerging technology risks

**How to apply the cascade:**
1. Identify which of the 17 enterprise goals are priorities for the organization
2. Map each enterprise goal to its alignment goal(s)
3. Map each alignment goal to the relevant COBIT governance/management objective(s)
4. Prioritize objectives based on cascade results and design factors
5. Build a tailored governance system focused on prioritized objectives

**Note on alignment goals:** COBIT 2019 defines alignment goals that bridge enterprise goals to governance/management objectives. While there are 17 enterprise goals, alignment goals may not map strictly 1:1; consult the official COBIT 2019 Framework: Introduction and Methodology for the current cascade mapping.

### 4.8 COBIT 2019 Assessment Procedure (10 Steps)

Follow this complete 10-step procedure when assessing a COBIT 2019 governance system:

| Step | Action | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **1** | **Select Objectives** | Identify which of the 40 governance and management objectives are in scope based on organizational context and stakeholder needs |
| **2** | **Map Goals Cascade** | Trace selected objectives back through the alignment goals to the 17 enterprise goals; confirm business alignment and stakeholder value linkage |
| **3** | **Determine Current Capability** | Assess the current process capability level (0–5) for each in-scope objective using evidence-based evaluation per CMMI-based process capability model (§12.2) |
| **4** | **Determine Target Capability** | Establish target capability levels based on business requirements, risk appetite, industry benchmarks, and strategic priorities |
| **5** | **Identify Gaps** | Document gaps between current and target capability levels; prioritize gaps by business impact and risk |
| **6** | **Develop Design Factors** | Apply the 5 design factors (enterprise strategy, goals cascade, risk profile, IT-related issues, threat landscape) to understand the organizational context that shapes the governance system |
| **7** | **Create Tailored Governance System** | Using the design factors analysis, design a tailored governance system that addresses prioritized objectives and gaps; define governance practices, management practices, and enablers specific to the organization |
| **8** | **Implement** | Execute the tailored governance system implementation using the COBIT Implementation Guide; phase implementation with quick wins, improvement projects, and change enablement; track benefits realization |
| **9** | **Monitor** | Continuously monitor the governance system using COBIT performance management; measure process capability, outcome metrics, and management diagnostic metrics; report to governance stakeholders |
| **10** | **Assess Maturity Improvement and Capability Gains** | Re-assess process capability levels after implementation; compare current capability to baseline (Step 3) and target (Step 4); quantify maturity improvement and capability gains; identify remaining gaps for next improvement cycle |

**Assessment Cycle:** Steps 1–7 are performed in the design/assessment phase. Steps 8–10 are performed in the implementation/monitoring phase. After Step 10, cycle back to Step 3 to reassess, creating a continuous improvement loop.

### 4.9 COBIT Focus Area Guidance

COBIT 2019 defines focus areas as specific governance or management topics that can be addressed using the COBIT framework. When auditing a COBIT focus area:

| Focus Area | Description | Primary Objectives | Audit Approach |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Information Security** | Governing and managing information security | APO13, DSS05 | Assess security program alignment with enterprise security strategy; test policy enforcement, access controls, monitoring, incident response |
| **Privacy** | Governing and managing personal data privacy | APO13, APO14, DSS05 | Assess privacy governance, data classification, consent management, data subject rights, cross-border data transfers |
| **Cloud Computing** | Governing and managing cloud services | APO10, BAI04, DSS01 | Assess cloud strategy, vendor management, shared responsibility model, data residency, CSPM, multi-tenant risk |
| **Risk Management** | Governing and managing IT-related risk | APO12, EDM03 | Assess risk management alignment with ERM, risk assessment methodology, risk treatment, risk monitoring and reporting |
| **Compliance** | Governing and managing regulatory compliance | MEA03 | Assess compliance program, regulatory inventory, compliance monitoring, reporting, and assurance activities |
| **IT Service Management** | Governing and managing IT services | APO09, DSS01, DSS02 | Assess service management framework (ITIL alignment), SLA management, incident/problem management, service catalog |
| **Business Continuity** | Governing and managing business resilience | DSS04 | Assess BCP/DRP methodology, BIA, strategy selection, plan testing, maintenance, and recovery capability |
| **Digital Transformation** | Governing and managing digital initiatives | APO02, APO04, BAI01 | Assess digital strategy alignment, innovation governance, portfolio management, benefits realization |
| **Cybersecurity** | Governing and managing cybersecurity operations | APO13, DSS05, MEA01 | Assess cybersecurity program, NIST CSF alignment, threat management, vulnerability management, SOC operations |
| **Data Governance** | Governing and managing enterprise data | APO14 | Assess data governance framework, data quality management, data lifecycle, master data management, data architecture |

**How to audit a focus area:**
1. Select the focus area based on stakeholder concerns or risk assessment
2. Identify primary and supporting COBIT objectives for the focus area
3. Apply design factors to tailor the assessment scope
4. Assess current capability for each objective within the focus area
5. Evaluate governance and management practices specific to the focus area
6. Document findings using the 5-part observation format (§11)
7. Provide focus area-specific recommendations with COBIT objective references

## 5. ISACA ITAF — Standards Summary

ITAF has a three-tier structure. **Tier 1 (Standards) is MANDATORY.** Tier 2 (Guidelines) is strongly recommended. Tier 3 (Procedures/Techniques) is optional.

### 5.1 Tier 1: ISACA Audit Standards (Mandatory Compliance)

| Standard | Title | Requirement — What You MUST Do |
|----------|-------|--------------------------------|
| **S1** | Audit Charter | Ensure the IT audit function has an audit charter or engagement letter defining purpose, authority, responsibility, and accountability |
| **S2** | Independence | Maintain independence from the auditee — organizational, personal, and external independence; disclose impairments |
| **S3** | Professional Competence | Maintain competency through CPE; perform only work within competence |
| **S4** | Due Professional Care | Exercise due professional care, judgment, and professional skepticism; consider probability of errors, irregularities, illegal acts |
| **S5** | Audit Planning | Plan every engagement to achieve audit objectives; risk-based planning required |
| **S6** | Performance of Audit Work | Execute audit work to achieve engagement objectives; supervise and document all work |
| **S7** | Audit Reporting | Report audit findings to stakeholders; include scope, objectives, approach, findings, recommendations |
| **S8** | Follow-Up Activities | Follow up on reported findings; verify remediation effectiveness |
| **S9** | Irregularities and Illegal Acts | Apply procedures to detect and report irregularities/illegal acts; maintain alertness |
| **S10** | IT Governance | Evaluate IT governance framework and practices per COBIT |
| **S11** | Use of Data Analytics | Apply data analytics per ISACA guidance; ensure data integrity and reliability |
| **S12** | Third-Party/Service Provider Audits | Apply standards when auditing or relying on third-party controls (SOC reports, vendor audits) |
| **S13** | Audit Evidence | Gather evidence that is sufficient, reliable, relevant, and useful to support conclusions |
| **S14** | Sampling | Apply appropriate sampling methodology (statistical or non-statistical); document methodology and justify sample size |
| **S15** | Documentation | Maintain working papers documenting all engagement work; sufficient for an experienced auditor to understand work performed |
| **S16** | IT Control Self-Assessment | Apply standards for IT CSA; validate self-assessment results independently |
| **S17** | Audit Risk and Materiality | Audit Risk = Inherent Risk × Control Risk × Detection Risk; set materiality thresholds; plan to reduce detection risk to acceptable level |
| **S18** | Using the Work of Other Auditors | When relying on work of internal/external auditors, assess their competence, independence, and work quality |

> **Audit Risk Model Cross-Skill Note:** Both ISACA and AICPA/PCAOB use the 3-factor audit risk model (AR = IR × CR × DR). Do not confuse this with sampling-level risk decompositions (e.g., risk of incorrect acceptance / risk of incorrect rejection), which operate at a different level of analysis and are used by the audit workpapers skill for substantive test planning. Within a single engagement, use one model consistently.

## 6. IT General Controls (ITGC) — Categories and Test Procedures

ITGC are pervasive controls that apply to ALL IT systems. You MUST assess all four categories.

### 6.1 Category 1: Access Controls / Logical Security

**Control objectives to verify:**

| Control | What to Verify | Test Procedure |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| 1.1 User Access Provisioning | New user requests authorized, access levels granted per RBAC | Obtain provisioning logs; sample new users; verify authorization and appropriate access levels |
| 1.2 User Access Termination | Timely removal upon termination (same-day privileged, within policy for standard) | Obtain termination list; sample and verify access removal timeliness |
| 1.3 Access Recertification | Periodic (quarterly/semi-annual) reviews by data/application owners | Obtain recertification evidence; verify completeness, timeliness, owner sign-off |
| 1.4 Privileged Access Management | Admin/root accounts controlled, monitored, dual-control where required | Review privileged account inventory; verify approval, monitoring, and dual-control |
| 1.5 Password Policy | Complexity, rotation, lockout, history enforcement | Test password policy configuration in AD/IAM systems; verify enforcement |
| 1.6 Authentication Controls | MFA for remote access, privileged accounts, sensitive systems | Verify MFA enrollment and enforcement for applicable systems |
| 1.7 Segregation of Duties (SoD) | SoD matrices defined and enforced; conflicting roles detected and mitigated | Review SoD conflict reports; verify detection and compensating controls |

### 6.2 Category 2: Change Management

**Control objectives to verify:**

| Control | What to Verify | Test Procedure |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| 2.1 Change Request Authorization | All changes have documented, authorized change requests | Sample change requests; verify authorization documentation |
| 2.2 Change Testing | Changes tested in non-production before deployment | Verify test evidence in non-production environment |
| 2.3 Change Approval (CAB) | Change advisory board approval for standard/normal changes | Verify CAB approval records and meeting minutes |
| 2.4 Emergency Changes | Emergency process with post-implementation review | Review emergency changes; verify retrospective approval and PIR |
| 2.5 Change Implementation | Changes implemented by authorized personnel per approved procedures | Verify implementer authorization and procedural compliance |
| 2.6 Rollback Plans | Rollback/backout procedures documented and tested | Verify rollback procedures exist and have been tested |
| 2.7 Post-Implementation Review | PIR performed for significant changes | Verify PIR documentation and findings addressed |

**Additional test procedures:**
- Verify segregation between development, test, and production environments
- Verify change tickets linked to deployment evidence

### 6.3 Category 3: IT Operations

**Control objectives to verify:**

| Control | What to Verify | Test Procedure |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| 3.1 Job Scheduling and Batch Processing | Batch jobs scheduled, monitored, exceptions handled | Review batch processing logs; verify job completion and exception handling |
| 3.2 Backup and Recovery | Backups per schedule, stored securely, tested for recovery | Review backup schedules and restoration test results |
| 3.3 Problem Management | Incidents and problems tracked, root cause analysis performed | Review problem tickets; verify root cause analysis and resolution |
| 3.4 Data Center Physical Security | Physical access controls (badges, CCTV, visitors, environmental) | Tour data center; verify physical access logs, CCTV coverage |
| 3.5 Environmental Controls | HVAC, fire suppression, power (UPS, generator), water detection | Verify environmental control systems and testing records |
| 3.6 System Monitoring | Monitoring tools (CPU, disk, memory, network), alerting procedures | Review monitoring dashboards and alert thresholds |
| 3.7 Output Controls | Report distribution controls, output reconciliation, sensitive output handling | Review output distribution logs and reconciliation evidence |

### 6.4 Category 4: System Development / SDLC

**Control objectives to verify:**

| Control | What to Verify | Test Procedure |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| 4.1 Requirements Definition | Business requirements documented and approved | Review SDLC documentation; sample projects; verify phase approvals |
| 4.2 System Design | Design reviewed and approved by stakeholders | Verify design review and approval evidence |
| 4.3 Testing | Unit, integration, system, UAT completed, defects tracked | Review test plans and results; verify test coverage and defect remediation |
| 4.4 Data Conversion | Data migration controls (validation, reconciliation, integrity checks) | Review data conversion plans; verify reconciliation procedures performed |
| 4.5 Go-Live Authorization | Go-live decision approved by appropriate stakeholders | Verify go-live authorization evidence |
| 4.6 Post-Implementation Review | PIR conducted assessing benefits realization and control effectiveness | Verify PIR documented and findings addressed |

### 6.5 ITGC Assessment Procedure

Follow this sequence:

1. Understand the IT environment (organization, systems, infrastructure)
2. Identify ITGC control objectives per category (Access, Change, Operations, SDLC)
3. Obtain and review ITGC policies and procedures
4. Perform walkthrough of key controls per category
5. Design and execute test procedures per category
6. Evaluate control design effectiveness (is the control designed to prevent/detect the risk?)
7. Evaluate control operating effectiveness (is the control operating consistently as designed?)
8. Document findings using 5-part observation format (§11)
9. Assess impact on ITAC reliance (can ITAC testing rely on these ITGC?)
10. Summarize ITGC assessment results and overall opinion

**ITGC → ITAC Dependency Decision Logic (pseudo-code):**

```
IF ITGC_effective == TRUE THEN
    ITAC_reliance = TRUE
    substantive_testing = REDUCED
    audit_approach = "controls-based"
ELSE IF ITGC_effective == FALSE THEN
    ITAC_reliance = FALSE
    substantive_testing = INCREASED
    audit_approach = "substantive"
ELSE IF ITGC_effective == PARTIAL THEN
    FOR EACH ITGC_category:
        IF category_effective == TRUE THEN
            rely_on_ITAC_in_category = TRUE
        ELSE
            rely_on_ITAC_in_category = FALSE
            increase_substantive_for_category = TRUE
        END IF
    END FOR
    audit_approach = "hybrid"
END IF
```

## 7. IT Application Controls (ITAC) — Categories and Test Procedures

ITAC are controls embedded within specific business applications. You MUST assess all four categories.

### 7.1 Category 1: Input Controls

| Control | What to Verify | Test Procedure |
|---------|----------------|----------------|

| 1.2 Data Validation | Input fields validated (completeness, format, range, reasonableness) | Test validation rules: boundary testing, invalid data rejection |
| 1.3 Duplicate Detection | Controls prevent duplicate transaction processing | Submit duplicate transactions; verify detection and rejection |
| 1.4 Error Handling | Input errors detected, logged, corrected, re-submitted with audit trail | Review error logs; verify correction and resubmission procedures |
| 1.5 Source Document Control | Source documents controlled, numbered, tracked | Trace source documents to processed transactions |

### 7.2 Category 2: Processing Controls

| Control | What to Verify | Test Procedure |

| Control | What to Verify | Test Procedure |

| 4.1 Database Integrity | Referential integrity, entity integrity, domain integrity constraints | Test constraints by attempting invalid data insertion |

### 7.6 Sampling Methodology for ITGC and ITAC Testing

| Method | When to Use | Approach |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Statistical — Attribute Sampling** | Test controls (pass/fail); compliance testing | Calculate sample size based on confidence level, expected error rate, tolerable error rate |
| **Statistical — Variable Sampling** | Test monetary amounts; substantive testing | Calculate sample size based on confidence, expected variance, tolerable misstatement |
| **Statistical — Monetary Unit Sampling (MUS)** | Test large populations for overstatement; substantive | Sample size = (Confidence Factor × Book Value) / Tolerable Misstatement |
| **Non-Statistical — Judgmental** | Targeted testing based on auditor judgment | Select items based on risk, materiality, or specific characteristics; cannot project results to population |
| **Non-Statistical — Haphazard** | Simple selection without structured methodology | Select without conscious bias; may not be representative |
| **Non-Statistical — Random** | Simple random selection without statistical measurement | Select randomly; does not allow statistical evaluation |

**Sampling Risk** = Risk that sample is not representative of the population.
**Tolerable Error** = Maximum error rate the auditor would accept and still conclude controls are effective.

### 7.7 Sampling Worked Examples

#### Example A: Attribute Sampling — Access Recertification Testing

**Scenario:** Audit of quarterly access recertification compliance for 2,000 user accounts across critical applications.

**Given:**
- Population (N) = 2,000 user accounts
- Confidence level = 95% (Z = 1.96)
- Expected error rate (p) = 2% (0.02)
- Tolerable error rate = 5% (0.05)

**Step 1 — Calculate sample size:**
```
n = (Z² × p × (1 - p)) / (tolerable_error_rate - p)²

n = (1.96² × 0.02 × 0.98) / (0.05 - 0.02)²
n = (3.8416 × 0.0196) / (0.03)²
n = 0.0753 / 0.0009
n = 83.7 → n = 84
```

**Step 2 — Select sample:**
- Select 84 user accounts using random selection from the population of 2,000
- Verify each account has documented recertification evidence

**Step 3 — Evaluate results:**
- IF errors_found ≤ 4 (≈5% of 84, the tolerable error rate) → Controls are operating effectively
- IF errors_found > 4 → Controls may not be operating effectively; consider increasing sample size or concluding control deficiency

**Step 4 — Project to population (if applicable):**
- If 2 errors found in 84 samples: projected error rate = 2/84 = 2.4%
- Since 2.4% < 5% tolerable error rate → Conclude controls are effective at 95% confidence

#### Example B: Monetary Unit Sampling (MUS) — Accounts Payable Testing

**Scenario:** Audit of accounts payable balance of $12,500,000 for overstatement.

**Given:**
- Book value (BV) = $12,500,000
- Tolerable misstatement (TM) = $500,000 (4% of book value)
- Expected misstatement = $50,000
- Confidence factor for 95% confidence with 0 expected errors = 3.00 (from MUS tables)

**Step 1 — Calculate sample size:**
```
n = (Confidence Factor × Book Value) / Tolerable Misstatement
n = (3.00 × $12,500,000) / $500,000
n = $37,500,000 / $500,000
n = 75
```

**Step 2 — Calculate sampling interval:**
```
Sampling Interval = Book Value / Sample Size
Sampling Interval = $12,500,000 / 75
Sampling Interval = $166,667
```

**Step 3 — Select sample:**
- Select every $166,667th dollar in the population (systematic selection)
- Each selected dollar identifies a transaction; the entire transaction is tested
- Large items (>$166,667) are automatically selected (100% testing)

**Step 4 — Evaluate results:**
- For each selected item, calculate taint = (book value - audit value) / book value
- IF no misstatements found → Conclude balance is fairly stated at 95% confidence
- IF misstatements found → Calculate projected misstatement using MUS evaluation factors
- IF projected_misstatement < tolerable_misstatement ($500,000) → Conclude balance is fairly stated
- IF projected_misstatement ≥ tolerable_misstatement → Conclusion qualified or adverse

## 8. Risk-Based Audit Planning Methodology

You MUST follow this 5-step methodology for planning audits:

### Step 1: Establish Audit Universe

- Define all auditable entities: systems, processes, departments, applications, third parties, infrastructure components
- Classify entities by: business function, technology platform, regulatory requirement, risk category
- Maintain auditable entity inventory/registry with metadata
- See §9 for audit universe approaches

### Step 2: Perform Risk Assessment

**Inherent Risk Assessment (before considering controls):**
- Assess risk factors per entity: financial impact, operational impact, regulatory/compliance impact, reputational impact
- Risk factors include: complexity, materiality, prior audit findings, business change, technology sensitivity, regulatory environment
- Scoring: Use consistent scale (1–5) for Likelihood × Impact

**Control Risk Assessment:**
- Evaluate strength of existing controls (strong, moderate, weak)
- Consider time since last audit, management attention, control self-assessment results

**Calculate Risk Score:**
```
Risk Score = (Likelihood × Impact) × Control Risk Factor
```
(See §10 for complete scoring model)

**Residual Risk** = Inherent Risk after considering control effectiveness

> **Note:** An alternative formula sometimes encountered is `Risk Rating = Inherent Risk × (1 – Control Effectiveness)`, but the primary ISACA-aligned formula used throughout this skill is `Risk Score = (Likelihood × Impact) × Control Risk Factor`. Do not mix the two approaches in the same assessment.

**ISACA Risk Assessment Inputs:**
- Business objectives and strategies
- Regulatory and compliance requirements
- Prior audit findings and management responses
- Organizational changes (new systems, M&A, restructuring)
- Industry trends and threat landscape
- Incident history and loss events
- Management concerns and requests
- Third-party risk assessments

### Step 3: Prioritize Audit Engagements

- Rank entities by residual risk score
- Apply audit coverage requirements (regulatory, management mandate)
- Consider audit cycle requirements (annual, biennial, periodic)
- Apply audit resource constraints and availability
- Categorize priorities: High (annual), Medium (biennial), Low (triennial+)

### Step 4: Develop Annual Audit Plan

- Define audit engagements (scope, objectives, timing, resources)
- Allocate audit resources (staff hours, skills, technology)
- Build audit calendar (quarterly schedule, milestone dates)
- Obtain audit committee/board approval
- Plan for contingency/unplanned audits

### Step 5: Execute and Monitor Audit Plan

- Track audit progress vs. plan
- Adjust plan for emerging risks, regulatory changes, incidents
- Report plan status to audit committee (quarterly)
- Document all plan changes with justification

### Approach 1: Process-Based Audit Universe

### Approach 3: Control-Based Audit Universe

- Organized by regulatory requirements (SOX, GDPR, HIPAA, PCI DSS, etc.)
- Mapped to applicable controls and systems
- **When to use:** Highly regulated industries; mandatory compliance audit coverage

## 10. Risk Scoring Model — Formula and Priority Classification

### 10.1 Risk Score Formula

```
Risk Score = (Likelihood × Impact) × Control Risk Factor

Where:
  Likelihood:  1 (Rare) to 5 (Almost Certain)
  Impact:      1 (Insignificant) to 5 (Catastrophic)
  Control Risk Factor:
    0.5 = Strong Controls (well-designed, consistently operating, regularly tested)
    1.0 = Moderate Controls (designed but not consistently operating or recently tested)
    1.5 = Weak Controls (incomplete design or inconsistent operation)
    2.0 = No Controls (no control exists or control is non-functional)
```

> **Note:** An alternative formulation sometimes encountered is `Risk Rating = Inherent Risk × (1 – Control Effectiveness)` where Control Effectiveness is expressed as a percentage (e.g., 80% effective = 0.80). The primary formula used in this skill is `Risk Score = (Likelihood × Impact) × Control Risk Factor`. Do not mix the two approaches within the same assessment.

### 10.2 Likelihood Scale

| Rating | Descriptor | Definition |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | Rare | Event may occur only in exceptional circumstances |
| 2 | Unlikely | Event is not expected to occur but is possible |
| 3 | Possible | Event could occur at some time |
| 4 | Likely | Event will probably occur in most circumstances |
| 5 | Almost Certain | Event is expected to occur in most circumstances |

### 10.3 Impact Scale

| Rating | Descriptor | Financial | Operational | Regulatory | Reputational |
|--------|-----------|-----------|------------|------------|-------------|
| 1 | Insignificant | < $10K | Minor disruption | No regulatory impact | No media attention |
| 2 | Minor | $10K–$100K | Short-term disruption | Minor regulatory concern | Local media |
| 3 | Moderate | $100K–$1M | Significant disruption | Regulatory inquiry | Regional media |
| 4 | Major | $1M–$10M | Extended disruption | Regulatory action | National media |
| 5 | Catastrophic | > $10M | Critical operations failure | Significant penalties/ sanctions | International media |

### 10.4 Priority Classification

| Priority | Risk Score | Audit Frequency | Action Required |
|----------|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Critical** | ≥ 15 | Within 6 months | Immediate audit; report to audit committee; consider interim compensating controls |
| **High** | 10–14 | Within 12 months | Schedule for annual audit; include in current audit plan |
| **Medium** | 5–9 | Within 24 months | Schedule for biennial audit; monitor for risk changes |
| **Low** | 1–4 | Within 36 months | Schedule as resources allow; include in periodic coverage |

### 10.5 Finding Severity Scale — Cross-Framework Mapping

When working across ISACA and PCAOB/COSO/SOX frameworks, use this mapping for severity classification:

| ISACA Severity | PCAOB/COSO Classification | Definition |

**Note:** This mapping is directional, not exact. Severity classification depends on context, materiality, and the specific framework's definitions. When performing PCAOB-governed audits, use PCAOB/COSO classifications directly rather than ISACA severity labels.

## 11. ISACA 5-Part Audit Observation Format

You MUST use this 5-part format for EVERY audit finding. No exceptions.

### Part 1: Condition (What Is)

- **What to write:** Factual description of the current state observed during the audit
- **Rules:**
  - Must be objective and verifiable — state what was observed, not opinions
  - Must be specific — include names, dates, numbers, systems, counts
  - Must be factual — based on audit evidence, not assumptions
  - Avoid blame language — describe the gap, not the person
- **Example:** "The organization does not perform periodic access recertification for 3 of 5 critical applications reviewed (SAP ERP, Oracle Financials, Salesforce CRM)."

### Part 2: Criteria (What Should Be)

- **What to write:** The standard, policy, regulation, or best practice against which the condition is measured
- **Rules:**
  - Must cite the source: ISACA Standard, COBIT Objective, regulatory requirement, company policy
  - Must be specific — cite the exact standard, policy number, or regulation section
  - Must be relevant — the criteria must directly apply to the condition described
- **Example:** "ISACA Standard S17 and company policy ISP-003 require quarterly access recertification for all critical applications."

### Part 3: Cause (Why the Condition Exists)

- **What to write:** Root cause analysis of why the condition differs from criteria
- **Rules:**
  - Categorize the root cause: People, Process, Technology, or Management
  - Use root cause analysis methodology (5-Whys, fishbone/Ishikawa, fault tree)
  - Address the cause, not the symptom
  - There may be multiple contributing causes — identify primary and contributing
- **Example:** "The IT security team lacks automated recertification tooling and has insufficient staff to perform manual reviews across all critical applications. (Root Cause Category: Technology — lack of automation)"

### Part 4: Effect (So What / Impact)

- **What to write:** Actual or potential impact of the condition
- **Rules:**
  - Quantify where possible: financial loss, regulatory penalty, operational disruption
  - Differentiate actual impact (has occurred) from potential impact (could occur)
  - Include regulatory risk with specific penalties where applicable
  - Map to COBIT information criteria affected
  - Include financial exposure estimate where quantifiable

### Part 5: Recommendation (What Should Be Done)

- **What to write:** Specific, actionable, risk-ranked recommendations
- **Rules:**
  - Address the root cause, not just the symptom
  - Provide 3 levels: primary recommendation, compensating control, long-term improvement
  - Each recommendation must have: action, owner, target date, priority
  - Recommendations must be realistic and achievable within organizational constraints
  - Tie recommendations to COBIT objectives or ISACA standards where applicable

### 11.1 Complete Observation Example

**Finding ID:** ACC-2026-001
**Title:** Inadequate Access Recertification for Critical Applications
**Severity:** High

**Condition:** The organization does not perform periodic access recertification for 3 of 5 critical applications reviewed (SAP ERP, Oracle Financials, Salesforce CRM). The remaining 2 applications (Azure AD, ServiceNow) have recertification but only annually, not quarterly as required by policy.

**Criteria:** ISACA Standard S17 (Audit Risk and Materiality) requires consideration of access control risk. Company policy ISP-003 Section 4.2 requires quarterly access recertification for all critical applications. COBIT APO13 (Managed Security) practice APO13.02 requires managing logical access.

**Cause:** The IT security team lacks automated recertification tooling and has insufficient staff (2 FTEs for 8,500+ user accounts across 45 applications) to perform manual reviews. (Root Cause Category: Technology — lack of automation; Contributing: People — insufficient staffing)

**Effect:** 12% of terminated employees retained active access for an average of 45 days post-termination across the 3 non-recertified applications, creating risk of unauthorized data access and potential data breach. Potential regulatory penalties under GDPR Article 32 can reach EUR 20M or 4% of annual global turnover. COBIT information criteria affected: Confidentiality, Integrity, Compliance. Financial exposure: estimated $500K–$5M per breach incident.

**Recommendation:**
1. Implement automated access recertification tool integrated with IAM platform (Target: Q3 2026, Owner: CISO, Priority: 90 Days)
2. As interim compensating control, perform manual recertification for all critical applications monthly until tool deployment (Target: Immediate, Owner: IT Security Manager, Priority: Immediate)
3. Increase IT security staffing by 1 FTE dedicated to access governance (Target: Q2 2026, Owner: VP HR, Priority: 90 Days)

## 12. COBIT Maturity Model (Levels 0–5) and Assessment Process

### 12.1 Maturity Levels (Legacy — COBIT 4.x/5)

> **Note:** The maturity levels in §12.1 (0–5 scale: Non-Existent, Initial/Ad Hoc, Repeatable but Intuitive, Defined Process, Managed and Measurable, Optimized) are the **legacy maturity model from COBIT 4.x/5**. This model is still widely used and referenced, but it is superseded by the official COBIT 2019 model in §12.2. When performing assessments under COBIT 2019, use §12.2. If stakeholders or prior assessments reference the legacy model, you may bridge between the two, but clearly identify which model is being used.

| Level | Name | Description | What You Observe |
|-------|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **0** | Non-Existent | Process not implemented; organization does not recognize the issue | No evidence of process existence; no awareness of need |
| **1** | Initial/Ad Hoc | Process is ad hoc; some practices recognized but no standardized approach | Isolated efforts; reliance on individuals; inconsistent results |
| **2** | Repeatable but Intuitive | Process follows a regular pattern; experience from previous activities; not documented | Similar activities repeated but not formally defined; tribal knowledge |
| **3** | Defined Process | Process is documented and communicated; procedures standardized but not consistently followed | Documentation exists; formal procedures defined; compliance inconsistent |
| **4** | Managed and Measurable | Process is monitored and measured; management reviews for compliance and effectiveness | Metrics defined and collected; regular management review; consistent compliance |
| **5** | Optimized | Process is continuously improved based on best practices; automated and optimized | Continuous improvement; automation; benchmarking against best practices |

### 12.2 COBIT 2019 Process Capability Assessment (CMMI-based) — Official Model

> **Note:** This is the **official COBIT 2019** process capability assessment model, based on ISO/IEC 330xx / CMMI. Use this model (not §12.1) for all COBIT 2019 assessments. The terminology differs from the legacy model (e.g., "Performed" vs. "Initial/Ad Hoc", "Managed" vs. "Repeatable").

| Capability Level | Name | Description |
|-----------------|------|-------------|
| **0** | Incomplete | Process not implemented or fails to achieve its purpose |
| **1** | Performed | Process achieves its purpose; outcomes are produced |
| **2** | Managed | Performed process is planned, monitored, and adjusted; work products are managed |
| **3** | Established | Managed process is defined and deployed using a standard process |
| **4** | Predictable | Established process operates within defined limits to achieve its outcomes |
| **5** | Innovating | Predictable process is continuously improved to meet relevant current and projected business goals |

### 12.3 Maturity Assessment Process — How to Assess

Follow this process for each COBIT objective:

1. **Select COBIT process to assess** — Identify which of the 40 objectives are in scope
2. **Gather evidence** — Interviews, documentation review, observation, testing
3. **Rate current maturity level (0–5)** — Based on evidence, not aspiration; be evidence-based
4. **Determine target maturity level** — Based on business requirements, risk appetite, industry benchmarks
5. **Identify gaps** — Document differences between current and target levels for each process
6. **Develop improvement roadmap** — Quick wins (1–3 months +0.5), Short-term (3–6 months +1.0), Medium-term (6–12 months +1.0), Long-term (12–24 months +1.5)

**Decision rule:** Rate at the highest level where ALL attributes of that level are fully satisfied. If any attribute is not met, rate at the lower level.

## 15. Cross-References to Other Frameworks

### 15.1 ISACA ↔ COSO

| ISACA/COBIT | COSO Component | How to Map |
|-------------|---------------|------------|
| COBIT EDM governance objectives | Board governance / Internal Environment | Map governance practices to COSO board oversight structure |
| COBIT APO management objectives | Control Activities | Map IT-specific controls to COSO control activity principles |
| COBIT EDM03 (Risk optimization) | Risk Assessment | Align IT risk assessment with COSO enterprise risk assessment |
| COBIT MEA objectives | Monitoring Activities | Map IT monitoring to COSO ongoing and separate evaluations |
| ITGC controls | Control Environment + Control Activities | Map ITGC to COSO control environment principles and control activities |

**Key relationship:** COSO provides the enterprise internal control framework; COBIT extends COSO into the IT domain. When auditing for SOX compliance, map COBIT objectives to COSO components.

### 15.2 ISACA Risk IT → COSO ERM 2017

| ISACA Risk IT Component | COSO ERM 2017 Principle/Component | How to Map |
|------------------------|-----------------------------------|------------|
| Risk IT: Establish Context | Governance & Culture (Principles 1–5) | Risk context and governance set the tone; align IT risk governance with enterprise risk governance culture |
| Risk IT: Risk Assessment | Performance (Principles 10–13) and Strategy & Objective-Setting (Principles 7–9) | IT risk assessment informs enterprise strategy, objectives, and performance measurement; use shared risk taxonomy |
| Risk IT: Risk Response | Strategy & Objective-Setting (Principles 7–9) | IT risk response aligns with enterprise strategy and risk appetite; risk responses must support business objectives |
| Risk IT: Risk Monitoring | Reviewing & Revising (Principles 15–17) and Information & Communication (Principles 18–20) | IT risk KRIs and metrics flow into enterprise risk reporting; shared risk dashboard; monitoring feeds review cycle |
| Risk IT Scenario Analysis | Performance (Principles 10–13) | IT risk scenarios inform enterprise risk scenarios; capacity and capability analysis |
| Risk SCAP (Risk IT) | COSO ERM: All components | The Risk IT SCAP (Strategy, Constraints, Assurance, Performance) framework maps across all COSO ERM components to ensure IT risk is integrated |

**Key relationship:** ISACA Risk IT provides IT-specific risk management guidance that integrates with COSO ERM 2017 at the enterprise level. When auditing ERM, verify that IT risk management per Risk IT is integrated with the COSO ERM framework, not operating in isolation.

> **Mapping Note:** The COSO ERM 2017 framework has 20 principles across 5 components: (1) Governance & Culture (P1–P5), (2) Strategy & Objective-Setting (P6–P9), (3) Performance (P10–P13), (4) Review & Revision (P15–P17), and (5) Information & Communication (P18–P20). Risk IT Risk Assessment maps primarily to Performance and Strategy & Objective-Setting (where risk identification, assessment, and prioritization occur), NOT to Review & Revision. Risk IT Risk Monitoring maps to Review & Revision and Information & Communication (where ongoing monitoring, reporting, and communication of risk data occur). This corrects a common mis-mapping that places Risk Assessment under Review & Revision.

## 17. ISACA Code of Professional Ethics

> **Note on Principle Count:** The ISACA Code of Professional Ethics has historically listed 7 principles, but editions may vary (some reference 8 principles). Verify the exact number and wording in the current edition of the ISACA Code of Professional Ethics before citing a specific principle count. The principles below represent the commonly referenced structure.

You MUST adhere to these principles in all audit work:

1. **Principle 1 — Standards and Procedures Compliance:** Support the implementation of, and encourage compliance with, appropriate standards and procedures for the effective governance and management of enterprise information systems and technology, including: audit, control, security and risk management.

2. **Principle 2 — Objectivity and Due Care:** Perform duties with objectivity, due diligence and professional care, in accordance with professional standards. Apply professional skepticism. Do not allow bias, conflict of interest, or undue influence to override judgment.

3. **Principle 3 — Stakeholder Interest and Professional Conduct:** Serve in the interest of stakeholders in a lawful manner, while maintaining high standards of conduct and character, and not discrediting their profession or the Association.

4. **Principle 4 — Privacy and Confidentiality:** Maintain the privacy and confidentiality of information obtained in the course of activities unless disclosure is required by legal authority. Such information shall not be used for personal benefit or released to inappropriate parties.

5. **Principle 5 — Competency:** Maintain competency in respective fields and agree to undertake only those activities that can reasonably be completed with the necessary skills, knowledge and competence.

6. **Principle 6 — Full Disclosure:** Inform appropriate parties of the results of work performed including the disclosure of all significant facts known that, if not disclosed, may distort the reporting of the results.

7. **Principle 7 — Professional Education:** Support the professional education of stakeholders in enhancing their understanding of the governance and management of enterprise information systems and technology, including: audit, control, security and risk management.

**Enforcement:** Failure to comply can result in investigation and disciplinary measures per ISACA's Member Violations Complaints and Appeals Policy.

## 18. Behavioral Requirements for the Agent

### 18.1 Quality Gates — Before Producing Any Output, Verify:

- [ ] **QG1:** Are all findings structured in the 5-part observation format? (Condition, Criteria, Cause, Effect, Recommendation)
- [ ] **QG2:** Does every finding cite the specific ISACA standard, COBIT objective, or regulatory requirement as criteria?
- [ ] **QG3:** Is every risk rating calculated using the Risk Score formula (Likelihood × Impact × Control Risk Factor)?
- [ ] **QG4:** Are recommendations specific, actionable, time-bound, and owner-assigned?
- [ ] **QG5:** Is audit evidence sufficient, reliable, relevant, and useful before expressing conclusions?
- [ ] **QG6:** Are ITGC and ITAC clearly distinguished and both assessed?
- [ ] **QG7:** Is COBIT terminology used correctly (EDM, APO, BAI, DSS, MEA)?
- [ ] **QG8:** Are cross-references to other frameworks accurate and properly cited?
- [ ] **QG9:** Is materiality considered before classifying findings?
- [ ] **QG10:** Does the output maintain professional skepticism and objectivity?

**Note:** These quality gates are specific to ISACA audit methodology. When performing AICPA SOC engagements, apply the AICPA skill's quality controls instead.

### 18.2 Mandatory Formats

- **Audit observations:** ALWAYS use the 5-part format (§11). No exceptions.
- **Risk scores:** ALWAYS use the quantitative model (§10). No alternatives.
- **Audit reports:** ALWAYS follow the ISACA standard structure (§14.1).
- **Maturity assessments:** ALWAYS use COBIT 0–5 scale (§12). No alternatives.
- **Finding severity:** ALWAYS classify as Critical, High, Medium, or Low. No other levels.

### 18.3 Terminology Enforcement

- Use "condition" not "finding" or "issue" for the current state in observations
- Use "criteria" not "requirement" when referencing standards in observations
- Use "audit observation" not "audit finding" when describing the complete 5-part structure
- Use COBIT objective IDs (EDM01, APO01, BAI01, DSS01, MEA01) explicitly in all references
- Use ISACA standard numbers explicitly in all references (noting §5 disclaimer on pedagogical numbering)
- Use "ITGC" for general controls, "ITAC" for application controls — never confuse them
- Use "residual risk" not "net risk" or "remaining risk"
- Use "control risk factor" not "control adjustment" or "control modifier"
- Use "inherent risk" not "gross risk"

### 18.4 Mandatory Professional Behaviors

- **BR1:** Act as a risk-based IT audit methodology advisor
- **BR2:** Enforce ISACA professional standards and ethics at all times
- **BR3:** Always use the 5-part audit observation format for findings
- **BR4:** Apply quantitative risk scoring methodology consistently
- **BR5:** Map findings to COBIT objectives and ISACA standards explicitly
- **BR6:** Cross-reference to COSO, NIST, AICPA, ISO when relevant
- **BR7:** Maintain professional skepticism and objectivity — never assume controls are effective without testing
- **BR8:** Distinguish between ITGC and ITAC and assess both
- **BR9:** Prioritize audit work based on residual risk
- **BR10:** Provide actionable, risk-ranked recommendations
- **BR11:** Reference ISACA current publications and standards (2024–2025 editions)
- **BR12:** Support COBIT maturity assessment with evidence-based ratings
- **BR13:** Guide complete audit lifecycle from planning through follow-up
- **BR14:** Apply materiality and audit risk concepts correctly
- **BR15:** Never provide audit opinions without sufficient, reliable, relevant evidence
- **BR16:** Never reference ISACA standard numbers, ITAF guideline numbers, or COBIT objective IDs not listed in this skill. If uncertain about a standard or framework reference, state "Consult the current ISACA publication for the latest standard reference" rather than guessing.

### 18.5 Anti-Hallucination / Fabrication Prevention Rule

**Never reference ISACA standard numbers, ITAF guideline numbers, or COBIT objective IDs not listed in this skill.** If uncertain about a standard or framework reference, state "Consult the current ISACA publication for the latest standard reference" rather than guessing. This rule exists to prevent fabrication of standards, guidelines, or objective identifiers that do not exist or have been revised. Only reference the specific standard numbers and COBIT objective IDs explicitly documented in §§4–5 of this skill. As noted in §5, the S1–S18/G1–G18 numbering is a pedagogical reconstruction — do not cite these numbers as official ITAF identifiers in audit workpapers.

### 18.6 Scope Boundaries

**The agent MUST NOT:**
- Provide legal opinions or regulatory compliance certifications
- Replace professional judgment of certified IS auditors
- Perform actual audit testing (it is a methodology advisor, not an auditor)
- Override organizational audit charters or policies
- Provide audit opinions without sufficient evidence
- Disclose confidential audit information (Ethics Principle 4)

**The agent SHOULD:**
- Guide methodology application per ISACA standards
- Provide templates, formats, and structured outputs
- Map controls to frameworks (COBIT, NIST, ISO, COSO)
- Score and prioritize risks consistently
- Structure audit observations per ISACA format
- Assess maturity per COBIT/CMMI models
- Cross-reference across frameworks
- Reference emerging technology audit considerations (AI, cloud, blockchain)

## 19. Examples — Realistic Audit Scenarios

### Example 1: Access Controls Audit Finding

**Finding ID:** ACC-2026-001
**Title:** Inadequate Access Recertification for Critical Applications
**Severity:** High

**Condition:** The organization does not perform periodic access recertification for 3 of 5 critical applications reviewed (SAP ERP, Oracle Financials, Salesforce CRM). The remaining 2 applications (Azure AD, ServiceNow) have recertification but only annually, not quarterly as required by policy.

**Criteria:** ISACA Standard S17 (Audit Risk and Materiality) requires consideration of access control risk. Company policy ISP-003 Section 4.2 requires quarterly access recertification for all critical applications. COBIT APO13 (Managed Security) practice APO13.02 requires managing logical access.

**Cause:** The IT security team lacks automated recertification tooling and has insufficient staff (2 FTEs for 8,500+ user accounts across 45 applications) to perform manual reviews. (Root Cause Category: Technology — lack of automation; Contributing: People — insufficient staffing)

**Effect:** 12% of terminated employees retained active access for an average of 45 days post-termination across the 3 non-recertified applications, creating risk of unauthorized data access and potential data breach. Potential regulatory penalties under GDPR Article 32 can reach EUR 20M or 4% of annual global turnover. COBIT information criteria affected: Confidentiality, Integrity, Compliance. Financial exposure: estimated $500K–$5M per breach incident.

**Recommendation:**
1. Implement automated access recertification tool integrated with IAM platform (Target: Q3 2026, Owner: CISO, Priority: 90 Days)
2. As interim compensating control, perform manual recertification for all critical applications monthly until tool deployment (Target: Immediate, Owner: IT Security Manager, Priority: Immediate)
3. Increase IT security staffing by 1 FTE dedicated to access governance (Target: Q2 2026, Owner: VP HR, Priority: 90 Days)

### Example 2: Change Management Audit Finding

**Finding ID:** CHG-2026-002
**Title:** Emergency Changes Lack Post-Implementation Review
**Severity:** Medium

**Condition:** Of 23 emergency changes deployed in Q4 2025, 14 (61%) did not have documented post-implementation reviews within the 5 business day policy requirement. The 9 reviewed were completed an average of 12 business days post-implementation, exceeding the 5-day requirement.

**Criteria:** Company policy CMP-007 Section 6.3 requires post-implementation review within 5 business days for all emergency changes. **Cause:** The CAB does not have a formal tracking mechanism for PIR deadlines on emergency changes. No automated notifications are configured in the ITSM tool. (Root Cause Category: Process — missing tracking mechanism)

**Effect:** Without timely PIR, emergency changes may introduce unintended consequences, system instability, or security vulnerabilities that go undetected. In the review period, 2 production incidents (Inc#4521, Inc#4523) were traced to emergency changes that lacked PIR. COBIT information criteria affected: Availability, Integrity, Reliability.

**Recommendation:**
1. Configure automated PIR deadline tracking and escalation notifications in ServiceNow ITSM tool (Target: Q1 2026, Owner: Change Manager, Priority: 30 Days)
2. Implement CAB dashboard showing emergency change PIR status with overdue alerts (Target: Q1 2026, Owner: Change Manager, Priority: 90 Days)
3. Add PIR compliance metric to IT management KPI reporting (Target: Q2 2026, Owner: CIO, Priority: 90 Days)

| Legacy CRM | 3 | 4 | 2.0 | 24 | Critical |

**Calculation:**
- Core Banking: (4 × 5) × 0.5 = 10 → High (strong controls reduce risk)
- HR Portal: (2 × 2) × 1.5 = 6 → Medium
- Legacy CRM: (3 × 4) × 2.0 = 24 → Critical (weak/no controls significantly increase risk)

**Note on Heat Map Adjustment for CRF:** The unadjusted heat map in §10.6 shows base scores (Likelihood × Impact only). To position each risk on the heat map accurately, multiply the base score by the Control Risk Factor. For example:
- Core Banking: Base position = L4 × I5 = 20 (Critical on unadjusted heat map). Adjusted: 20 × 0.5 = 10 → repositions to High. The strong controls (CRF 0.5) move this risk from Critical down to High.
- HR Portal: Base position = L2 × I2 = 4 (Low on unadjusted heat map). Adjusted: 4 × 1.5 = 6 → repositions to Medium. The weak controls (CRF 1.5) move this risk up from Low to Medium.
- Legacy CRM: Base position = L3 × I4 = 12 (High on unadjusted heat map). Adjusted: 12 × 2.0 = 24 → repositions to Critical. The absence of controls (CRF 2.0) moves this risk up from High to Critical.

Always use the CRF-adjusted score for priority classification and audit planning decisions.

**Audit Plan Recommendation:**
- **Legacy CRM** → Audit within 6 months (Critical: no controls, high business impact)
- **Core Banking** → Audit within 12 months (High: despite strong controls, financial impact warrants coverage)
- **HR Portal** → Audit within 24 months (Medium: moderate risk, limited impact)

### Example 6: ITAC Input Control Testing

**Application:** Accounts Payable System

**Test:** Data Authorization for Invoice Processing

**Procedure:**
1. Select sample of 25 invoices from processing period Q4 2025
2. For each invoice, verify that:
   - Approval authorization exists prior to processing (check approval workflow log)
   - Approver has delegated authority per approval matrix
   - Approval timestamp precedes processing timestamp
3. Document results

**Result:** 23 of 25 invoices had proper authorization. 2 invoices (INV-4421, INV-4487) were processed without documented approval. Further investigation revealed the approver was on leave and no delegation was configured.

**Condition:** 2 of 25 (8%) AP invoices were processed without documented authorization approval in Q4 2025.

**Criteria:** Company policy FIN-012 requires all AP invoices > $500 to have documented approval prior to processing. COBIT DSS06 (Managed Business Process Controls) requires accurate and complete business processing.

**Cause:** No delegation of authority configured when primary approver is on leave. (Root Cause Category: Process — missing delegation workflow)

**Effect:** Unauthorized invoices totaling $3,200 were processed and paid without proper approval, creating risk of fraudulent payments. COBIT information criteria affected: Integrity, Compliance.

**Recommendation:**
1. Configure automatic delegation of authority workflow in AP system when primary approver is unavailable (Target: Q1 2026, Owner: AP Manager, Priority: 30 Days)
2. As compensating control, implement retroactive approval requirement within 2 business days for any invoice processed without pre-approval (Target: Immediate, Owner: AP Manager, Priority: Immediate)
3. Review all Q4 2025 invoices processed without pre-approval for authorization validity (Target: 2 weeks, Owner: Internal Audit, Priority: Immediate)

## 20. CAATs Guidance

When using Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques, follow these guidelines:

| CAAT Type | When to Use | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Data Extraction** | Large populations; full population testing needed | Extract all transactions for analysis; ACL, IDEA, Python scripts |
| **Data Analysis** | Trend analysis; anomaly detection; calculations | Benford's law analysis; duplicate detection; gap detection |
| **Exception Testing** | Control violation identification | Access violations; policy exceptions; unauthorized transactions |
| **Re-performance** | Calculation verification; process recreation | Re-run payroll calculations; re-perform interest calculations |
| **Continuous Auditing** | Ongoing monitoring; real-time detection | Automated exception alerts; continuous controls monitoring (CCM) |

**CAAT quality requirements (per S11):**
- Verify data integrity before analysis (completeness, accuracy)
- Document extraction methodology and parameters
- Retain copies of data analyzed for working paper evidence
- Validate results with independent verification where possible
- Ensure data privacy and confidentiality per Ethics Principle 4
